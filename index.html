Understanding
Community Development
Districts

Before you close on any Florida property, understanding the CDD is one of the most important — and most overlooked — steps in your due diligence. Here is everything you need to know.

What is a CDD?
Community Development District

A CDD is a special-purpose local government entity created under Florida Statute Chapter 190. It allows a developer to finance community infrastructure — roads, utilities, drainage, and amenities — using tax-exempt municipal bonds, then pass repayment of those bonds to property owners over time through their annual tax bills, typically over 20 to 30 years.

How CDD Charges Appear
Component One
Debt Service

Repayment of the original infrastructure bonds. This is the larger of the two charges and disappears once the bonds are fully paid off — typically 20 to 30 years from community inception.

Component Two
Operations & Maintenance

Ongoing upkeep of CDD-managed amenities and infrastructure. Unlike the debt service, this portion remains on the tax bill indefinitely — it does not expire when the bonds are paid.

Advantages & Considerations
Advantages
World-class infrastructure from day one

CDDs allow developers to build resort-caliber amenities and infrastructure that would otherwise take decades to fund incrementally — pristine roads, pools, fitness centers, trails, and landscaping.

Lower financing cost passed to buyers

Tax-exempt municipal bond financing carries a lower interest rate than private borrowing — theoretically benefiting the end buyer compared to if the developer financed infrastructure privately.

Professional long-term maintenance

The O&M portion funds a dedicated management structure for common areas. Communities tend to remain well-maintained long term, protecting property values.

Resident governance over time

CDD boards are elected by property owners as the community matures, transferring control away from the developer and giving owners meaningful input over budgets and operations.

Bond payoff flexibility

Outstanding bond balances can be paid off in a lump sum at or after closing, eliminating the debt service portion from future tax bills — a significant long-term cost saving many owners overlook.

Considerations
Significant ongoing cost

CDD assessments in Florida communities can range from $1,500 to over $4,000 per year — sometimes considerably more — depending on the community and remaining bond balance. This materially affects true cost of ownership.

It never fully disappears

Even after debt service bonds are retired, the O&M assessment remains permanently. Property owners are enrolled in the district for as long as they own within it.

Inflates true cost of ownership

A property that appears attractively priced may carry a CDD assessment that significantly raises the actual annual cost. This must be factored into any return on investment or cash flow analysis.

Newest communities carry highest balances

Buyers entering newly developed communities take on the full bond obligation at its maximum. The debt service burden is heaviest in the early years and decreases over time as principal is repaid.

Governance can become contentious

As communities mature and residents assume CDD board control from the developer, disagreements over assessment levels, capital projects, and management decisions can create friction and uncertainty.

Key Insight for Investors

Many property owners carry the full outstanding bond balance for years without realizing they could pay it off in a lump sum at closing. Doing so eliminates the debt service portion of the CDD assessment from the tax bill going forward — often resulting in meaningful annual savings. When evaluating any CDD property, always request the current outstanding bond balance and factor the payoff option into your acquisition analysis.

For the Investor
What This Means for Your Portfolio
Net operating income is directly affected. The CDD assessment is an owner expense that must be accounted for in any cash flow or cap rate calculation. Always model it explicitly.
Rent pricing must reflect it. CDD costs factor into the rent needed to achieve target returns. Properties in CDD communities typically command — and require — higher rents to maintain investor margins.
Hold vs. sell decisions are impacted. A property with a high remaining CDD bond balance and declining rent growth may reach a crossover point where sale — and bond payoff from proceeds — produces a better outcome than continued holding.
Always request the CDD estoppel certificate. This document confirms the current annual assessment, outstanding bond balance, and payoff amount. It is a standard part of Florida real estate due diligence and should be reviewed before any offer is finalized.
